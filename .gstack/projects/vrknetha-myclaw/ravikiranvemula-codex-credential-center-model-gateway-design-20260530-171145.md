## Problem Statement

The existing investor brief (`docs/Gantry_Investor_Brief.html`) positions Gantry as
"the enterprise agent runtime," a horizontal platform an enterprise would operate. The
founder's actual business, and his own words, contradict that: "Gantry is not a SaaS
platform." KnackLabs builds vertical, domain-specific AI applications (tender discovery,
customer service) for enterprises, and Gantry is the proprietary runtime/SDK that makes
those apps governed, trustworthy, and fast to ship.

The brief is therefore pitching the wrong company. It files KnackLabs next to horizontal
agent-infra players (LangChain, Vellum) where it looks early and underfunded, instead of
next to vertical AI application companies where it has real, paid, pre-launch traction and a
defensible engine. The brief is honest and well-built; it just sells the engine instead of
the business the engine powers.

## Status Quo

Enterprises want AI agents but cannot specify them upfront. Agents are labor, not software:
you cannot write the job description before they start working. The normal build process
(spec, build, ship) fails because requirements are undefinable, behavior is nondeterministic
(same persona + different memory = different behavior), and there is no audit trail. Most
enterprise agent projects stall here. The workaround today is either no agents at all, or
ungoverned prototypes that never reach a regulated production function.

## Pricing Model (researched + recommended)

Founder instinct: one-time build fee + license. That is the right backbone and matches the
enterprise norm (implementation fee + annual license). The 2026 market adds a usage/outcome
layer: hybrid (base + usage) is now standard (≈41% of AI vendors per Bessemer's 2026 AI
Pricing Playbook, up from 27% in 2025; hybrid vendors report materially higher net revenue
retention). Recommended three layers:

1. **One-time implementation / build fee** — the bespoke first build per client. Services
   margin. *Shrinks per client as the vertical productizes* (Gantry + template do more of the
   work). This shrink is the margin bend. Benchmark: enterprise AI implementation commonly
   $25K–100K; Gartner pegs implementation at 2.5–4x license.
2. **Annual platform license** — recurring, predictable. Product margin. Benchmark: enterprise
   AI contracts often start ~$50K/yr; outcome-AI comps run higher (Sierra ~$150K+/yr, Decagon
   median ~$400K/yr).
3. **Usage / outcome layer** — for tender, a natural high-value unit: per tender matched, per
   bid prepared, or per tender *won*. A won tender is worth crores to the client, so per-bid
   or per-win pricing is an easy ROI sell and captures real value. Benchmark: outcome pricing
   ~$1–3.50 per resolution in support; tender outcomes are far higher value, price up.

Practical sequencing: at one client in test, start with build fee + annual license (the
instinct). Add the usage/outcome lever once there is volume and measurement. Do not pitch
pure outcome-based yet. For the brief, present all three as the model and draw §6 from layer 1
(shrinking) vs layers 2–3 (growing).
