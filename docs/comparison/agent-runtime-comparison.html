Agent Runtime Comparison

Four ways to run an AI agent.
Only one is built to put inside your product.

OpenClaw, NVIDIA's NemoClaw, Microsoft's Scout, and Gantry all run autonomous agents. The difference isn't the AI. It's the runtime around it, the part that decides whether an agent is safe to trust with real work, real data, and real customers.

What this means if you're deciding
  • Every option here can be made safe. The real question is whether safety is on by default or left to your team to assemble.
  • OpenClaw and NemoClaw are excellent for one person on one machine. Scout and Gantry are built for a whole company.
  • Only one is a building block you drop into your own product, with the safety already wired in.

The honest verdict

▲ Where Gantry leads

Five wins that matter most when an agent meets a real business.
Safe by default, not by setup
The protections are on out of the box, not a checklist your team has to assemble.
Default-deny permissions, per-action approval, never a raw shell, and a supply chain that installs nothing without human review.
Your keys stay out of the AI's hands
The AI never holds your passwords or API keys, so a tricked agent has nothing to leak.
Credentials encrypted at rest; the agent gets only a short-lived token; model calls run through a host-side proxy that adds the real key at the network boundary.
Built for many people, not just one
Every user's data stays walled off, so it holds up for a whole company.
Memory isolation enforced in the database per app/agent/subject; approval gated to designated approvers per conversation.
A building block, not a closed app
Drop it into your own product through an API, and your logins and roles stay in charge.
API-first runtime: SDK + control API drive agents and approvers; bring-your-own identity; Slack / Teams / Telegram / your app.
Runs anywhere, no lock-in
Not tied to one vendor's cloud or operating system.
Node + Postgres on any OS; provider-neutral channels. For anyone not all-in on one vendor, that's the whole game.

▼ Where others lead

No spin. Each of these is a real strength of a competitor.
Raw isolation: NemoClaw & Scout
Their sandboxes are kernel-grade and deny-by-default; Gantry's is strong, but best-effort on macOS.
OpenShell (Landlock/seccomp/netns) and MXC are deny-by-default kernel sandboxes; Gantry boxes the agent in at the OS level, strongest on Linux.
Total on-device privacy: NemoClaw
If your rule is that no data may ever leave the machine, NemoClaw's fully-local model is unmatched.
Local Nemotron inference + privacy router keep PII on-device; Gantry self-hosts state but usually calls a cloud model.
Built-in corporate identity: Scout
Microsoft brings a full company directory and single sign-on out of the box.
Entra identity, SSO, conditional access; Gantry instead plugs into the identity of the app you embed it in.
Big-vendor reporting: Scout
Microsoft ships fleet-wide alerting and threat intelligence.
Purview + Defender; Gantry keeps a full action log and leaves alerting to your own monitoring stack.

Two families, one split

OpenClaw and NemoClaw are made for one trusted person on one machine. Scout and Gantry are made for a company, where many people, real customer data, and accountability are all in play.

Personal · one machine

OpenClaw and NemoClaw. NemoClaw matches anyone on raw isolation and leads on pure on-device privacy. But it stops at the personal boundary.

Enterprise · your product

Scout and Gantry. Gantry carries the security all the way into a product you can ship: safe by default, walled off per user, embeddable, no lock-in.

And the isolation scales with you, toward complete, horizontally-scalable containment in the cloud, containerised as needed, as deployments grow.

The agent is the easy part. The runtime you can trust with a business is the hard part, and the one you can build on is rarer still.
